### Technical Guide for the Implementation of Section 16: "Other Information" in Safety Data Sheets (SDS)

##### 1\. Introduction and Strategic Significance of Section 16

In the architecture of chemical safety, Section 16 represents the final authoritative layer that provides context and technical continuity to the entire document. According to the "Double Utility" principle established in the INSST (NTP 1198), this section serves a dual purpose: it acts as a primary source for comprehensive risk analysis and as a functional tool for informing and protecting workers.For the Senior Industrial Safety Architect, Section 16 is the "operational bridge" between the raw laboratory toxicity data found in Sections 11 and 12 and the practical, shop-floor safety management protocols. Its strategic significance lies in ensuring  **Document Interoperability** —allowing safety professionals to interpret the SDS not as an isolated form, but as a dynamic component of the facility’s integrated safety management system. A high-quality SDS begins with meticulous document control and a transparent revision history.

##### 2\. Document Control, Revision History, and Responsibility

To maintain  **Legal Defensibility** , Section 16 must provide an audited trail of the document's lifecycle. This transparency is critical for verifying compliance with regulatory transition periods, such as the mandatory shift to GHS in Mexico on  **October 9, 2018** , under NOM-018-STPS-2015.

* **Revision Identification:**  Clearly distinguish between the initial preparation date and the latest revision (e.g., "Fecha de emisión/revisión: 15, Oct, 2014").  
* **Versioning Control:**  Reconcile external release numbers with internal versioning to avoid control lapses (e.g., "Release\# 8.00" vs. "Version 1.07").  
* **Specific Modifications:**  Itemize modified sections to alert users to shifts in safety protocols.  
* **Technical Responsibility:**  List the Name, Department, and Contact of the technical responsible party for direct inquiries.**The "So What?" Layer:**  Explicitly stating the "Reason for Revision"—whether due to the GHS 2018 mandate, new toxicological data, or regulatory updates—is a legal requirement under Art. 3 of RD 374/2001. Any modification in Section 16 necessitates a re-evaluation of the workplace risk assessment, as even minor technical corrections can alter the required Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) or exposure monitoring frequency.

**The "So What?" Layer:**  Categorizing these terms logically prevents meaning from being lost in numerical shorthand. For instance, distinguishing between a  **DNEL**  (safe level) and a  **DMEL**  (risk management for non-threshold substances) is vital for industrial hygienists setting internal safety margins.

##### 4\. Full-Text Synthesis of H and P Statements: Hazards and Precautions

Section 16 must expand all abbreviated codes to eliminate ambiguity during emergency response. This includes the synthesis of combined phrases and historical data layers.

* **Combined Hazard Statements:**  Specific focus must be placed on combined risks such as  **H302 \+ H332 (Nocivo en caso de ingestión o inhalación)** , which indicates concurrent exposure risks for substances like Benzyl Alcohol.  
* **Specific Health Hazards:**  Ensure specific organ toxicity is noted, such as  **H373 (oral)**  (May cause damage to organs through prolonged or repeated exposure) for components like 4,4'-methylenebis(cyclohexylamine).  
* **Physical Hazards:**  Anchor phrases to specific components; for example,  **H225**  (Highly flammable liquid and vapour) for  **Ethylbenzene**  vs.  **H226**  (Flammable liquid and vapour) for  **Xylene** .  
* **Historical Data Layer (R-Phrases):**  For facilities managing legacy stocks or transitioning from DSD/DPD, Section 16 should provide the full text of R-phrases (e.g.,  **R20/21/22** : Nocivo por inhalación, ingestión y en contacto con la piel;  **R52/53** : Nocivo para organismos acuáticos).**The "So What?" Layer:**  Providing the full text is critical for accurate label interpretation. Without the expanded text of combined phrases, a responder might overlook secondary routes of entry, leading to inadequate medical treatment or containment.

##### 5\. Bibliographic Foundations and Data Sources

The scientific authority of the SDS rests upon the quality of the cited sources. Citations establish the document’s legal and scientific defensibility.

* **Technical Guides (INSST):**  The bibliography must include specific NTP codes as foundations:  **NTP 871, 878, 880, 881**  (CLP/REACH basics),  **973, 974, 1059**  (Mixture classification), and  **1061**  (Exposure Scenarios).  
* **Regulatory Databases:**  References should include ECHA, HSDB (Hazardous Substances Data Bank), and the INSHT (Spain) exposure limit database.  
* **Manufacturer Data:**  Citation of raw material supplier SDS and official monographs (e.g., IARC) is mandatory to validate the classification of the final mixture.

##### 6\. Standardized Legal Disclaimer and Liability Limitations

A formal "Notice to Reader" defines the legal boundaries of the information provided, emphasizing the shift of responsibility once the product is in the user's control.**Notice to Reader / Disclaimer:**   *The information provided herein is based on our current knowledge at the date of issuance. It describes safety properties but does not constitute a guarantee of specific product traits. The user is solely responsible for determining product suitability for their application and complying with all local, state, and national regulations. Due to the*  ***proliferation of third-party information sources***  *(SDS aggregators and unofficial databases), the manufacturer is not responsible for SDS documents obtained from any source other than the official supplier. Unrecommended uses not specified in Section 1 are at the user's sole risk.***The "So What?" Layer:**  This disclaimer protects the supplier from liability arising from unauthorized SDS versions or modifications made by third-party aggregators, emphasizing that only the original document is valid for regulatory compliance.

##### 7\. Occupational Training and Safety Awareness: The Human Barrier

Documentation alone is insufficient; human competence is the ultimate "human barrier" in the safety system. Section 16 must detail the mandatory training areas required to transform the SDS into active protection.

* **SDS Interpretation:**  Personnel must be trained to interpret  **VLA-ED**  (Daily Exposure) and  **VLA-EC**  (Short-term) values to manage shift-based exposure.  
* **Hazard Mapping:**  Workers must understand why a substance classified with  **H317**  (Skin sensitization) requires the specific gloves (e.g., Nitrile/EN374) specified in Section 8\.  
* **Emergency Competence:**  Training must cover the immediate first-aid response for corrosives (H314) and the management of static discharge in flammable environments (H226).

##### 8\. Miscellaneous: National Peculiarities and Safety Optimization

Section 16 is the correct location for regional requirements that facilitate international trade and safety optimization.

* **National/Regional Peculiarities:**  
* **WGK (Germany):**  Water Hazard Class (e.g., WGK 1, 2, or 3).  
* **MAL-CODE (Denmark):**  Volatility and skin risk index (e.g., 3-1).  
* **APQ (Spain):**  Storage requirements for chemical products.  
* **ABM (Netherlands):**  General Evaluation Methodology for water discharge.  
* **Optimization Tools:**  Inclusion of  **SUMI**  (Safe Use Mixture Information) or  **LCID**  (Lead Component Identification) for downstream users.**The "So What?" Layer:**  Section 16 must  **never**  be used for data that legally belongs in Sections 1–15. It is a proactive space for safety optimization, clarifying complex requirements like exposure scenarios and ensuring that the safety message remains clear across international jurisdictions. Maintaining this section through proactive updates is not a clerical task—it is a commitment to continuous industrial safety improvement.
